The manifesto

No doctrine. No hierarchy. Just the longest honest version of what we're actually doing here, and why.

The honest story

There's no cosmology we're asking you to swallow. No origin myth, no afterlife you have to buy into, no secret that costs money to unlock.

Here's the actual story. Every tradition humans ever built — every religion, every philosophy, every grandmother's rule — said the same thing somewhere near its center: take care of each other. That part was never the problem. The problem was everything that got bolted on around it: the hierarchies, the gatekeepers, the lists of who's in and who's out, the people who figured out that a lonely person will pay almost anything to feel less alone.

We're not here to fix religion. We're not qualified to and we're not interested. We're here to do the one thing under all of it — be decent to each other, on purpose, consistently — and skip the rest.

Humans aren't good or bad by default. We're deeply shaped by who's around us and what we practice. The algorithms know this. So do the politicians. That's the whole game — shape what you practice, shape who you become. So if attention is being engineered to make us anxious, divided, and alone, then a small group practicing the opposite on purpose isn't soft. It's a counterweight.

You're not your worst era. You're not your highlight reel either. You're a work in progress, same as everyone else who finds their way here. That's the only thing you have to believe, and you don't even have to believe it — you just have to show up like it might be true.

How we move

The seven principles

Not rules. Not commandments. Just the things we keep coming back to — one paragraph each, because they deserve more than a bullet point.

01

We're all connected

Your situation is tied to your neighbour's — not metaphorically, actually. The person next to you doing badly makes your world worse, and you making things a little better for them makes yours better too. This isn't sentiment, it's just how communities work. So act like it: the wellbeing of the people around you is not separate from your own.

02

Everyone has worth

Not earned. Not conditional. Not dependent on output, follower count, productivity, or how interesting you are at a party. Just — yes, full stop. The moment worth becomes something you have to prove, you've recreated the exact machine we're trying to step out of. Here, the floor is the floor for everyone.

03

Actions over aesthetics

Posting about kindness isn't kindness. A nice grid isn't a nice life. What you actually do — especially when no one is watching, especially when it's inconvenient — is the only thing that counts. We'd rather have one person who quietly shows up than a thousand who perform showing up. The aesthetic is a byproduct, never the point.

04

Sit with "idk"

Not knowing is fine. Pretending to know is the problem. Most of the damage in the world gets done by people who are certain — about who's wrong, about who deserves what, about how it all works. Uncertainty is honest. "I don't know" is a complete sentence and often the truest one in the room. We make space for it instead of rushing to fill it.

05

Repair over cancel

People mess up. You will, everyone here will. The interesting question was never whether someone failed — it's whether they come back and make it right. Cancelling is easy and feels clean and changes nothing. Repair is slow, awkward, and actually fixes the thing. We choose the harder one, and we make it safe enough that people are willing to try.

06

Be here

Real presence in 2026 is genuinely rare, which is exactly why it lands so hard when you get it. Phone down, fully in the room, listening like the other person is the only thing happening — that's a kind of attention the whole economy is built to steal from you. Giving it freely, to the people in front of you, is one of the most countercultural things you can do.

07

You're enough

The entire system runs on convincing you that you're not — not thin enough, rich enough, productive enough, healed enough, optimized enough. We don't do that here. You don't have to become someone else to belong. You're already a full member of the species, already worth showing up for. Start from enough, and grow from there if you want to — but never to earn the right to be here.

What we're not

The anti-cult clause

Communities built on kindness can curdle into something controlling faster than anyone wants to admit. So we name the guardrails out loud, and we hold them harder than any belief:

No single person whose word is final. No guru, no founder-as-prophet, no inner circle that decides truth for everyone else. Roles rotate. The founder is anonymous specifically so there's no face to follow.

No gatekeeping who belongs. No required beliefs, no purity tests, no statement of faith you have to sign. You don't have to agree with everything written here. You don't even have to agree with most of it.

No us-versus-them. The moment a community defines itself by who it's against, it stops being a community and starts being a faction. We're not against anyone. We're just for something.

No monetising your loneliness. Your need to belong is not a revenue stream. If anyone ever starts treating it like one, that's the red flag — point at it.

And if someone here starts acting like they have all the answers, that is itself the warning sign. The health check is simple, and we run it constantly: does the quietest, newest, most different person in the room feel as safe as anyone else? If the answer is no, something's broken, no matter how good everything else looks.

Where this goes

The vision

The vision is almost embarrassingly small, and that's on purpose. We're not trying to scale to a billion users or convert the planet or build a movement with a capital M. We're trying to make it normal — boring, even — for small groups of people to take genuine care of each other, every week, indefinitely.

Picture thousands of circles, none of them famous, none of them led by anyone you've heard of, scattered across timezones and cities and group chats, each one quietly being the place where eight to twelve people are known and looked after. No central authority. No headquarters. Nothing to capture or corrupt or sell. Just a frequency a lot of people happen to be tuned to at once.

This document is living. It's v0.1. The community owns it, not the founder — so it'll change as the people in it change it. That's not a bug. That's the entire design.

We're not trying to save the world. We're trying to be decent to each other consistently — which, honestly, might be the same thing.
